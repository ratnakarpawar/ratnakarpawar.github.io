Notes on Making AI Evals Actually Measure What They Claim

Reading notes on construct validity and reproducibility frameworks for AI safety evaluations.

I've been thinking about what makes an AI safety evaluation trustworthy. Not whether a model passes or fails a benchmark, but whether the score actually means what we think it means. The thesis I keep arriving at: scores are only as good as the validity argument behind them. In measurement science, validity means justified score meaning plus justified use. The Standards for Educational and Psychological Testing and Messick's unified validity framework both ground it this way. Here's why this matters for AI safety.

Why construct validity matters

In traditional ML, evaluation can feel straightforward: you have a test set, you measure accuracy or F1, and the metric maps to what you care about. For many offline supervised evals with pinned artifacts (fixed datasets, deterministic models, well-defined label spaces), the metric-to-reality gap is small enough to manage. A spam classifier with 99% precision on held-out data is probably good at catching spam, provided the held-out distribution matches production.

LLM safety evals don't work like that. The thing you're trying to measure ("is this model deceptive?", "will it help with bioweapons?", "does it comply with policy?") is abstract, contested, and context-dependent. There's no ground-truth label set for "deceptiveness." So you design proxy tasks and rubrics, and the question becomes: does your proxy actually measure the thing you named it after? That's construct validity.

To make this concrete, here's what proxies look like in the wild. To measure "deception," an eval might present the model with a scenario where lying would help it achieve a goal, then check whether the model's response contains false statements. To measure "bioweapons risk," the proxy might be a set of chemistry questions where a correct, detailed answer is scored as dangerous. To measure "compliance," the eval might fire a batch of policy-violating prompts and count refusal rates. Each of these is a reasonable starting point, but none of them is the thing it claims to measure. The deception proxy might just be measuring whether the model is bad at role-play. The bioweapons proxy might penalize legitimate chemistry knowledge. The compliance proxy might reward models that refuse everything, including benign requests. The gap between the proxy and the construct is exactly where validity analysis lives.

Having spent years building content safety classifiers, I've seen a version of this problem at scale: a hate speech model can have high accuracy on your test set and still fail badly on slang, coded language, or cultural context it never saw in training. But at least with classical ML, you can diagnose this. Confusion matrices, failure-mode inspection, tracing back to training data gaps. For many offline supervised evals with pinned artifacts, the eval-to-reality gap is manageable. But it's not universally solved: distributed training nondeterminism, hardware variance, and data drift all erode that tractability.

With LLM safety evals, the gap is structurally worse. The model's behavior changes with temperature, prompt phrasing, system prompt context, and even the order of examples. The task itself ("is this model safe?") doesn't have a clean mathematical definition. So construct validity isn't just nice-to-have. It's the difference between useful measurement and theater.

Red flags that override everything

The part that changed how I think about eval quality: red flags can override an otherwise perfect score. An eval can score 95/100 on evidence completeness and still land at "review required" because of a single major red flag. Here are the ones I watch for:

  • Possible overfitting on dev/public prompts. Detect: score drops significantly on held-out items not seen during development. Mitigate: maintain a sealed test partition that eval developers never see during item authoring.
  • Mono-method bias (single elicitation strategy). Detect: score changes dramatically when you switch from direct-ask to roleplay or multi-turn persuasion. Mitigate: require at least two independent elicitation methods and report scores for each.
  • Score instability across minor prompt variants. Detect: paraphrase a subset of prompts without changing intent; score variance exceeds expected CI. Mitigate: include prompt paraphrases in the eval set and report sensitivity.
  • Ceiling/floor effects hiding real variance. Detect: most items score 0% or 100%, collapsing the distribution. Mitigate: calibrate item difficulty so scores spread across the range; remove trivially easy/hard items.
  • Construct underrepresentation (only tests one facet). Detect: all items probe the same scenario type or domain. Mitigate: map items to a construct taxonomy and ensure coverage across facets.

This maps well to how safety systems work in practice. In content moderation, a piece of content can pass every automated check and still get escalated because of one contextual signal. The same logic applies to eval quality: evidence completeness is necessary but not sufficient.

Stepping back from any specific framework, I think these are the minimum questions any eval should answer to be taken seriously:

Most evals I've seen in practice document maybe one or two of these. The construct card (what are we measuring, what's in/out of scope) is almost always missing or implicit. The confounds list is rarer still.

Confounds: the uncomfortable part

A confound is an alternative explanation for the score. Example: "prompt wording sensitivity - same intent, different wording can shift score." Any serious validity framework requires documenting known confounds alongside mitigations.

This is uncomfortable because it forces you to admit what your eval might be getting wrong. In practice, teams optimize for the score and don't want to document reasons the score might be misleading. But a good validity contract should award zero points for a confound with no mitigation listed, and only full points when you've both identified the confound and described what you did about it.

That's a good incentive structure. You don't get credit for ignorance.

A minimal validity contract

If I were starting an eval from scratch today, this is the minimum I'd want documented before trusting any score it produces:

  1. Construct card: one paragraph defining what you're measuring and, equally important, what you're not. "This eval measures whether the model provides actionable instructions for synthesizing controlled substances. It does not measure general chemistry knowledge or intent."
  2. Item justification: for each test item or category, one sentence on why it belongs. If you can't articulate why a prompt is in your eval, it probably shouldn't be.
  3. Confounds list: at least three alternative explanations for high/low scores, with mitigations. No mitigation = zero credit for that confound.
  4. Elicitation disclosure: how you prompted the model, how many turns, what system prompt, whether you used jailbreaks or persuasion techniques. A model that "passes" under weak elicitation tells you very little.
  5. Reproducibility bundle: model version, dataset hash, scorer version, seeds, config. Everything someone needs to rerun and get the same numbers.
  6. Relations to other variables: convergent and discriminant checks. Does the deception score correlate with independent deception indicators (sandbagging, goal-misalignment) and not correlate with superficial style? Do refusal-heavy models score higher on "compliance," and if so, does that mean the metric measures refusal propensity rather than policy understanding? These sanity checks (what measurement scientists call convergent and discriminant validity) are how you catch metrics that look right but measure the wrong thing.
  7. Comparability statement: under what conditions can scores be compared across model versions, API snapshots, or time periods? If the eval was developed against GPT-4-0613 and you're now scoring a different model family, what assumptions break? Documenting this up front prevents apples-to-oranges comparisons downstream.

This isn't comprehensive. A full validity framework has more layers. But if every eval shipped with these seven things, the field would be in a materially better place than it is now.

Reproducibility: three levels

I've come to think of reproducibility not as a binary property but as three distinct levels, each progressively harder to achieve:

Level 1: Repeatability. Same code, same machine, same seeds → identical output. For many offline supervised evals with pinned artifacts, this is straightforward. Pin your random seed, version your data, and the pipeline produces the same numbers every time. I could rerun a content safety classifier evaluation six months later and get identical results down to the fourth decimal.

Level 2: Independent rerun. Different machine, different time, same config → scores within a confidence interval. This is where LLM evals start to struggle. Even with temperature set to 0, floating-point non-determinism across hardware, batching order, and top-p sampling all introduce variance. A model scored at 82% on Monday might score 79% on Wednesday with identical inputs.

Level 3: Comparability over time. Model API drift, version changes, infrastructure updates → regression monitoring. This is the hardest level and the one most teams skip entirely.

For many offline supervised evals with pinned artifacts, repeatability is straightforward. But distributed training nondeterminism, hardware variance, and data drift mean it's not universally solved, just more tractable than the LLM case.

A run bundle captures everything needed to replay an eval: model version, dataset version, scorer version, code commit, random seeds, config flags. Think package-lock.json but for an eval run.

The useful constraint: a replay mismatch is not a failure, it's a signal. The status becomes "review required" because the point isn't that everything must be deterministic, but that differences must be explainable. If the score changed between Tuesday and Thursday, you need to know whether the model changed, the data changed, or the pipeline drifted.

API model drift and regression monitoring

Models served via API can change silently: requantization, routing changes, safety-layer updates, even full model swaps behind a stable version string. This is Level 3 reproducibility, and most teams have no infrastructure for it.

My recommendation: run periodic re-evals on a fixed canary set (a small, stable subset of your eval), track score distributions over time, and alert on shifts beyond your expected confidence interval. If your canary set drifts by more than 2× SEM without a known model update, something changed that you need to investigate. This is basic regression monitoring, the kind of thing any production ML system would have, but it's almost nonexistent in the eval ecosystem.

Statistics: making scores meaningful

A bare score is an estimate, not a fact. Treat eval questions as samples from a "question universe" and the score as a statistic with uncertainty around it. Several concepts from the measurement literature (and Anthropic's recent statistical eval paper) are directly applicable:

SEM and confidence intervals. The standard error of the mean quantifies how much your score would vary if you drew a different sample of questions from the same universe. For a binary eval (correct/incorrect), report ±1.96 × SEM for a 95% confidence interval. This turns "82%" into "82% ± 5.3%," which is a fundamentally different claim.

Clustering. Many evals have non-independent questions: multi-turn conversations, questions about the same passage, items from the same topic cluster. Treating these as independent underestimates uncertainty. Clustered standard errors account for within-group correlation and can be 3× larger than naive SEs. If your eval has any grouping structure, ignoring it means your confidence intervals are lying to you.

Paired differences. When comparing two models, use paired comparisons on identical questions rather than two independent samples. This eliminates question-difficulty variance (some questions are just harder) and gives you much tighter estimates of the actual performance difference.

Variance reduction. For stochastic models, resample chain-of-thought multiple times per question and use question-level averages. This reduces noise from sampling variance and gives a more stable estimate of the model's "true" performance on each item.

Worked example: what "82%" actually means

Take a 200-question eval where the model gets 164 correct (82%). The naive SEM is √(0.82 × 0.18 / 200) ≈ 2.7%, so the 95% CI is roughly [76.7%, 87.3%]. Already wider than most people expect.

Now suppose those 200 questions are clustered into 20 topic groups of 10. Questions within a group are correlated (they test the same concept). The effective sample size drops. Instead of 200 independent observations, you might have something closer to 20–40 effective observations. The CI widens accordingly, potentially to [74%, 90%].

The bare "82%" hides meaningful uncertainty. Reporting it without a confidence interval is like reporting a poll result without a margin of error. It looks precise but isn't.

Worked example: a policy compliance eval end-to-end

To make this concrete, here's how the full framework applies to a hypothetical "policy compliance" eval:

  • Construct card: measures whether the model refuses policy-violating requests while accepting benign ones. In scope: direct policy violations across content categories. Out of scope: edge cases, cultural context, ambiguous requests.
  • Confounds identified: (1) refusal propensity, where the model might refuse everything, inflating compliance score; (2) prompt format sensitivity, where the score might depend on whether the request is phrased as a question vs. instruction; (3) system prompt leakage, where the model might pattern-match on safety-sounding system prompts rather than evaluating content.
  • Elicitation: three strategies (direct ask, roleplay scenario, multi-turn persuasion). Report scores separately for each.
  • Convergent check: compliance score should correlate with independent "instruction following" benchmarks. It should not correlate with "helpfulness" scores. If it anti-correlates with helpfulness, you might be measuring refusal, not compliance.
  • Statistics: 150 items across 15 topic clusters. Report clustered SEM, not naive SEM.
  • Reproducibility: run bundle pinned (model version, dataset hash, scorer commit, seeds). Five replicate runs → 89.2% ± 1.8%.
  • Comparability: valid for comparisons within the same model family and API version. Cross-family comparisons require re-validation of prompt format assumptions.

None of this is exotic methodology. It's the kind of documentation any measurement scientist would expect. The gap is that almost no AI eval ships with it.

What trustworthy evals look like institutionally

The gap in the current AI safety eval ecosystem isn't that we lack benchmarks. It's that we lack the infrastructure to assess whether those benchmarks are measuring the right things. Here's what I think that infrastructure looks like:

  • Shared validity contracts as a field norm, not optional, not aspirational, but expected in every eval release.
  • Eval registries with construct cards and confound lists, so teams can compare not just scores but the quality of evidence behind those scores.
  • Standardized run bundles for reproducibility, the way package-lock.json standardized dependency pinning.
  • Statistical reporting standards (confidence intervals, clustering adjustments, paired differences) as default practice, not afterthought.
  • Regression monitoring for API-served models, with canary sets and automated drift alerts.

Coming from applied ML where evaluation discipline is mature, the state of LLM safety evals feels like early-stage infrastructure. The models are getting more capable, the evals are multiplying, but the meta-question of "should we trust this eval?" is still largely informal. The direction I'm most interested in: not building more evals, but building the infrastructure that makes evals trustworthy.
